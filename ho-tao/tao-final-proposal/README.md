# Final Proposal

## What I'm going to make

I will be continuing my WavGan project from my midterm, but using the Random Walk algorithm from my symbollic music generation assignment to decide the sequence of the melody.

## Why this project matters

Commercial AI models like Suno lack granular control and often default to a sterile sound dictated by their locked training data. WaveGAN solves this by allowing me to curate my own dataset, returning the composition control back to me. To sequence the final piece, I will organize the generated .wav files numerically and use a Random Walk algorithm to stochastically navigate and assemble the audio.


## How machine learning will be involved

A GAN RNN takes random noise as an input and generates sequential data. It retains memory of the previous outputs in the sequence, keeping a structure to follow over time.

The RNN aspect reviews both the authentic sounds from the dataset and the fake sounds generated by the the generator, outputting the probability of whether the data is real or fake. 

These two networks are locked in a competition, where the generator continuously refines the output and the detecting network updates its parameters to become better at identifying the fakes.

## What data, source material, tools, models, platforms, workflows do I plan to use

I will use Google Colab to run WaveGAN, paired with a custom script to segment the audio into short one-second clips.

Training machine learning models requires significant computational power. Colab provides cloud-based GPU processing, which bypasses local hardware limitations and ensures thetraining phase runs efficiently.

Unlike many audio models that convert sound into images (spectrograms) before processing, WaveGAN operates directly on raw audio waveforms. This makes it highly effective at capturing the specific textural and timbral nuances of the dataset, ensuring the generated audio retains its unique aesthetic.

WaveGAN trains on extremely short audio snippets, often around one second long (specifically, 16384 samples at 16kHz).Manually finding, cutting, and exporting nearly 11,000 individual .wav files in a DAW is an impossible task. So I will write a script that automates this extraction, processing the dataset of around 3 hours.

Then I will need to create script that loads the directory of the sequenced files. The script will use a random number generator to determine the next index, and I will also have a chance to tamper with the probabilities/weights (e.g. make it jump from 0500 to 0550 instead of 0499 or 0501).
