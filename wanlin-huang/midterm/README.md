# Midterm Proposal: Can You Tell If It's AI?
### A Blind Listening Survey at Berklee

---

## What I Want to Do

I want to make a blind listening survey where Berklee students listen to music and guess whether it was made by AI or a human. Instead of posting it online, I want to set it up like a small installation on campus — something more personal and local.

This idea actually started from a conversation with my friend. She's in the scoring department and she's been using AI to write music for indie games and AIGC films, and nobody has figured out it's AI. But the thing she told me that really stuck with me is: even when the whole composition is AI-generated, she always does the mixing herself. That made me think — where exactly is the line between AI and human in music? That's kind of what I want to explore.

---

## Survey Structure

The total survey will take about 5–6 minutes. Participants will listen to 5–6 short clips, each around 30 seconds to 1 minute long, and answer two short questions after each one.

For each track, the survey flows like this:

```
Listen to Track (30–60 sec)
      │
      ▼
Question 1 — First impression:
Do you think this track was made by AI or a human?
   ┌───────────┐
   │  AI-made  │
   └───────────┘
        │
   ┌───────────┐
   │Human-made │
   └───────────┘
        │
        ▼
Question 2 — Musical elements:
Which parts influenced your decision?
☐ Instrumentation  
☐ Vocals  
☐ Composition  
☐ Arrangement  
☐ Sound Design  
☐ Mixing  
        │
        ▼
Why? (optional, open response)
        │
        ▼
Next Track
```

**Estimated timing per track:**

| Step | Time |
|---|---|
| Listen to clip | 30–60 sec |
| Answer questions | 15–20 sec |
| Total per track | ~45–80 sec |

For 5–6 tracks, total time is approximately 5–6 minutes.

I like this structure because it separates the gut reaction from the more analytical judgment. The first question is instinctive — just yes or no. The second question makes people actually think about why, which I think will give more interesting data.

## How I'm Going to Do It

For the music, I want tracks from two sides.

For the human-made side, I'm going to reach out to Dadabots (they're Berklee alumni), a student from the MIT Media Lab, and Professor Jonathan who teaches an AI music production course here — maybe he knows students who'd want to be involved.

For the AI side, I'll use Suno to generate tracks in similar styles. If nobody gets back to me, I'll use publicly available AI-generated music instead.

I'm aiming for at least 30 responses from people on campus. One of the reasons I'm keeping this as a campus-only installation rather than posting it online is that some of the music contributors don't want their work publicly associated with AI — which is itself an interesting reflection of the cultural moment we're in.

---

## Questions, Concerns, and Stretch Goals

One thing I'm honestly a little worried about is the anti-AI feeling at Berklee. Some people here have really strong opinions about AI music, and I don't want that to affect whether people participate or how they answer. I'm going to frame it as research about human perception — not about whether AI music is good or bad — and keep everything anonymous.

I'm also not totally sure how to handle music that's in the gray area, like my friend's work — is that AI or human? I want the survey to capture that complexity somehow.

As a stretch goal, I'd love to compare results across different music styles to see if some genres are easier to identify. And depending on how it goes, maybe I'll think about sharing the findings more publicly.
